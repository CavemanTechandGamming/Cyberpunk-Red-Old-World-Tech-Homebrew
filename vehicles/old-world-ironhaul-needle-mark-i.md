# Old World Ironhaul Needle — Mark I

**Short description:** Pre-Collapse **Class 8** **needle-nose** **tractor**—**Bad Larry Long** on the radio, **coffin sleeper** by default, **double-stretched** frame, **dual saddle tanks**, and a **turbodiesel** **inline-six** that idles like distant thunder. **Mark I** is salvage truth: oil weeps where gaskets remember the old century, air brakes hiss in moral judgment, and the fifth wheel still locks when you need to move a continent.

## Long description

Before the Collapse, the **Ironhaul Needle** line was what interstates were paved for: aluminum cab, steel frame stretched twice for wheelbase and weight the law pretended not to see, hood long enough to hide a small apartment, and stack pipes that said the world waits for me.

**Factory spec** for this homebrew line is **rear tandem** drive (six tires on the ground that matter in the fiction), a **big-cam turbodiesel inline-six** in the Class 8 spirit, and—by default for this sheet—a **coffin sleeper** (narrow, tall Old World long-haul silhouette), **double-stretched frame** (wheelbase lengthened twice in the fiction for maximum road presence), and **dual saddle tanks** along the rails so range outlasts your patience. **Calendar year** is flavor (think **early-1960s American needle-nose conventional** stretched into something weigh-station nightmares are made of), not a stat in **2045**. **No trailer included**—this sheet is the **tractor** only.

Runners who love drama call rigs like this **Bad Larry Long**—too much truck, too much fuel, too much ego for one lane.

**Mark I** means honest rot: frame rust where salt won, cab and coffin fiberglass delamination where sun won, steering that negotiates on all that wheelbase, and a jake brake that still remembers gravity.

**Appearance:** Needle nose—long tapered hood, vertical stack or twin stacks, cab set back behind the front axle like the Old World intended, then miles of rail before the fifth wheel thanks to the double stretch. Coffin sleeper rides high and narrow behind the day cab—weathered striping, faded numbers, maybe a name painted by a steady hand and bad judgment. Dual tanks left and right, dented and strap-scarred. Chrome bumper and grille pitted; paint fleet red gone pink, petrol blue chalked, or white yellowed to cream. Fifth wheel plate scarred by every trailer that ever married it. Inside the cab: bench or buckets, dash metal and glass that rattle on rough asphalt. Coffin berth smells like diesel, old tobacco, and miles.
