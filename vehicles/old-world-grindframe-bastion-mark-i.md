# Old World Grindframe Bastion — Mark I

**Short description:** Pre-Collapse **full-frame**, **four-wheel-drive**, **turbodiesel** three-row hauler—the kind of rolling monument built when regulators still let you sell a **house-sized** cabin on a **truck spine**. From the **factory** (in this homebrew line) it wore **big steel off-road bumpers**—front brush guard, meaty rear step bumper, recovery points—before the first owner smacked a pole. Salvage Mark I units leak, rust, and drink fuel like it is still cheap, but they will still **flatten** a checkpoint gate if you ask nicely.

## Long description

Before the Collapse, the **Grindframe Bastion** line was the answer to a very Old World question: *how many adults, dogs, and pelican cases can we move without admitting we need a van?* **Three rows**—the rearmost one usable by people who actually have knees—**split-fold** cargo that swallowed **contractor gear** and **weekend exile** in the same breath, and a **rear aperture** big enough to load **sheet goods** without creative cursing.

**Factory spec** leaned on a **turbodiesel V8** and a **four-wheel-drive** transfer case: not clever, not apologetic—**torque** you felt through the wheel when the idle hunted, and **4WD** for mud lots, fire roads, and “we are not getting stuck behind the riot line.” Some lots offered **gas big-blocks**; the **diesel** ones are what **salvage yards** still fight over. If you need a **visual** anchor, think **early-2000s** **full-size** **American** SUV proportions and trim—**calendar year** is **flavor** for your table, not a stat in **2045**.

From the **showroom**, this homebrew treats the **Bastion** as **already off-road dressed**: **heavy factory steel bumpers** (not dainty plastic clips), **approach-friendly** faces, **tow** hooks you could **shackle**, and **ride height** that said **job site** even in suburbia. That hardware is **part of the silhouette**, not an aftermarket fantasy.

**Mark I** here means **salvage truth**: tired suspension, **biofilm** in the third-row cup holders, a **transmission** that negotiates in bad faith, **bumper** corners **rolled** from curbs you **deny**, and body lines **dented** like it has stories it will not tell. It is still **eight seats** of **“we are leaving the city tonight”** energy if you keep coolant in it.

**Appearance:** **Long box** silhouette—**flat hood**, **upright windshield**, **chrome** or **brushed trim** pitted where acid rain won. **Roof rack** channels are **factory**; the **optional** **ladder frame** on the back door is **pure owner lore**. **Bumpers** are **thick factory steel**—brush guard up front, meaty **step** surface out back—scuffed, rusted at the mounts, honest. Paint is **pearl white gone grey**, **forest green** sun-bleached to **dust**, or **corporate black** with **door dings** mapped like starfields. **Wheels** are **steel** with **hubcaps** missing one clip, or **aftermarket** alloys **curbed** on every spoke; tires might still be aggressive all-terrain rubber if someone cared once. Diesel soot dusts the tailpipe lip. Inside: **bench** second row, **split** third, **plastic** that **creaks** when you breathe. The **smell** is **old coffee**, **ozone** from a dying air blower, and **hope**.

**Cost:** **GM / table.** **Mark I** is **cheap-for-what-it-is** only if you ignore **fuel**, **parking**, and **maintenance**—price it like a **large** ground vehicle your table respects, not like wearable gear.
