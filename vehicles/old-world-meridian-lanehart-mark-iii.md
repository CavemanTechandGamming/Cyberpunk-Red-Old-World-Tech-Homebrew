# Old World Meridian Lanehart — Mark III

**Short description:** **Legendary-refit** **Meridian Lanehart**—**FWD** **V6** four-door myth with hardened subsystems, regulated power, and the quiet confidence of the car that was never supposed to be interesting. Still **front-wheel drive** in the baseline line; the fiction is traction management and trunk space, not a transfer case.

## Long description

**Mark III** is not “a nicer commuter.” It is what you build when you cherry-pick the best Old World parts still on pallets—limited-slip behavior up front that does not fight the wheel on wet on-ramps, cooling and oiling that forgive traffic sprints, chassis bracing and discreet armor under skin that still reads like a rental’s boring cousin, climate control that filters smoke like it is personal, and a **V6** map that pulls like the Collapse forgot to happen.

The silhouette stays honest: four doors, long trunk, tall glass—**calendar year** stays flavor, not a stat. Five seats for crew, groceries, or a folded long gun in the well; nobody mistakes it for a sports car until the wrong person is behind the wheel.

At the table, treat **Mark III** as a serious practical asset: expensive for a sedan, quiet in reputation even when the exhaust is tuned polite, and a magnet for anyone who recognizes pre-Collapse part numbers on a cam sensor.

**Appearance:** Paint is deep—gunmetal pearl, storm grey, or flat agency black with a ceramic feel. Glass is laminated thick; rubber sits flush. Wheels are period-correct in silhouette but modern in strength. Bumpers and plastic fascias integrate with the armored story without erasing the soft-wedge sedan your brain remembers. Inside: seats that do not stink, redundant power taps for gear, a trunk that still swallows. Wrong in the details if you know where to look.

**Cost:** **GM / table.** **Mark III** can be a major capital expense—on par with serious cyberware packages or a fixer’s favorite low-profile ride if your economy supports it. Calibrate openly.

**Suggested price:** 35,000 eb — *baseline for balance; change freely to fit your campaign.*
